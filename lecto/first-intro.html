General Introduction

As is part of the philosophy around which LanguaMunity was developed, the way this course works does not involve the stand-alone use of the software. Rather, the software is meant to be used as part of a learning process that relies heavily on immersion as well. In a sense, the immersion process of learning a language can be likened to being dropped into a deep river - and this program (together with proper reference materials) can be likened to a raft and a paddle.

Eventually, reading will prove to be a very useful tool in your immersion process. Reading in your target language can have many benefits - among the many of them being that it can supplement your conversational immersion in the event that you don't get enough of it. However, there is a bare minimun of conversational immersion that you must have for which no amount of reading, TV watching, or anything else can substitute. Fortunately, if you live in the United States, Spanish is probably the easiest language to get this conversational immersion in (with the possible exception of English, which you should already be fluent in if you are reading this document).

Despite the lack of official recognition, Spanish is as much a part of the USA as French is a part of our neighbor to the North, Canada. The process of this becoming so began early in our nation's history with the annexation of Florida, and continued with other events such as the annexation of Texas, California, and other places formerly part of Mexico. While it is true that an influx of immigration from Mexico increased the prevalence of Spanish in the USA, it is by no means the sole source of this.

Because of the place Spanish has in the fabric of the United States, finding conversational immersion opportunities should be easier than with other languages. However, if you can't find a good conversational immersion opportunity in-person, try at least to get an online immersion-opportunity using technologies such as VOIP. More information on how to do this will be made available here (albeit through a hyperlink) as soon as we have more specific suggestions to offer.

Nonetheless - being new to the language, there are certain things you need to be able to say in your target language as you jump into the deep waters of conversational immersion. You need to be able to excuse yourself for not really knowing the language yet - and be able to ask for certain considerations in light of your lack of knowledge of the language. LanguaMunity refers to the expressions you will need to know for this purpose as "Newbie Expressions". The first few lessons in this course will be dedicated to them (as is the case with any properly-composed LanguaMunity course).

Another thing you will need in this course from the very beginning and throughout the learning process is good reference materials. For this reason, there are three web-sites you should acquaint yourself with right now (all three of which have apps available both for Android and for IOS). These sites are Google Translate, SpanishDict, and WordReference. As soon as possible you should visit all three of these sites and get to know how to use them effectively. It is also recommended to install all three apps on your phone if you have a phone with which they are compatible. They will be even more useful when you start reading in Spanish - but will already be useful when your immersion is just conversational.

However - do not use these online tools, or any tools at all for that matter, to cheat while using this program's flashcards. As a matter of fact, try to make sure that all windows to these sites are closed when you're doing a flashcard session. This is very important, because if you cheat, then the one whom you are cheating is yourself.

Spanish Characters

One thing that can take the wind out of your sails before you even begin is having trouble typing those Spanish characters that just don't appear in the English language. The way to do this can depend on a number of factors - from what operating system you are using, to even how the preferences are set on your machine. However - there are several guides online on how to do this.

StudySpanish.com has a page dedicated to that - which contains instructions for a number of Windows platforms, as well as instructions for Macintosh users.
